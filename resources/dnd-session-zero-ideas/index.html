D&D Session Zero Ideas

A good session zero is the cheapest insurance a campaign can buy. An hour spent here saves a dozen awkward moments later — and turns five people with character sheets into a table that actually wants the same game. Here's what to cover.

Align on the kind of game

Before anyone rolls stats, agree on tone and stakes. Gritty survival or heroic high fantasy? Heavy roleplay or tactical combat? Lethal or forgiving? People imagine wildly different games behind the same word "D&D" — naming it out loud is the whole point of session zero.

Build characters in the same room

Characters made together are characters with reasons to travel together. Ask each player one question about another's hero — how do you know each other, why do you trust them, what do you hold against them — and the party arrives already woven into a story instead of five strangers in a tavern.

Set the tone with something they can see

Words set expectations, but an image sets mood. Opening session zero with a single cinematic shot of the world — the storm-lit city, the frozen frontier, the cursed valley they'll call home — tells players how this game should feel faster than any paragraph of description.

Agree on the table's lines

Five minutes on what's welcome and what's off the table — themes to avoid, a quiet way to pause a scene — costs nothing and protects the fun for everyone. A table that feels safe takes bigger creative swings.

End on a hook

Don't close session zero on logistics. Close on a question: a letter with no sender, a body that shouldn't be there, a debt that just came due. Leave players wanting the next session before they've left the first.
